Real Food for 100km Rides: What Actually Works

From gagging on gels to perfecting rice balls, here's what I actually eat on 100km rides.

Why I Ditched Energy Gels

I remember the exact moment I swore off energy gels for good. I was about 70km into a 100km ride, feeling decent, when I ripped open a packet of lemon-lime goo. One squeeze later, my stomach turned into a war zone. The sweetness was cloying, the texture like cold snot, and within ten minutes I was fighting nausea instead of a headwind. I’d been sold on the idea that gels were “performance fuel,” but my gut disagreed violently.

Don’t get me wrong—they work for some people. But for me, real food changed everything. No more artificial flavors, no more sticky wrappers littering my jersey pockets, and no more mid-ride digestive roulette. I started experimenting with actual meals on the bike, and my rides transformed. My energy levels stayed steady, my stomach stayed happy, and I actually looked forward to eating while pedaling. If you’ve ever felt that gel-induced dread, stick with me—I’ve got some real food hacks that actually work.

Bananas: The Messy but Reliable Choice

Let’s start with the OG of ride fuel: bananas. They’re cheap, they’re everywhere, and they pack a solid punch of potassium and fast-acting carbs. But let’s be real—they’re a mess. Ever tried peeling a banana while descending at 40km/h? I’ve ended up with mushy fruit all over my handlebars more times than I care to admit. The trick is to pre-peel them and stash them in a ziplock bag. Then you just nibble pieces off like a civilized human.

Here’s what works for me on a 100km ride:

  • Eat one banana every 45-60 minutes. That’s about 100 calories each, easy to digest.
  • Pair with a salty snack. Bananas are high in potassium but low in sodium. I’ll bring a small bag of salted almonds to balance electrolytes.
  • Don’t let them get too ripe. Overripe bananas turn into paste in your pocket. Go for firm, slightly green ones—they hold up better.

Bananas won’t win any style points, but they’ll keep you moving. Just remember to toss the peel in a trash can, not on the road. Nobody likes a litterbug.

Rice Balls: My Game-Changing Discovery

I stumbled onto rice balls after a ride with a Japanese cycling buddy. He pulled out a little foil-wrapped triangle of rice, seaweed, and salmon, and I thought he was crazy. One bite later, I was hooked. These things are pure fuel—simple carbs, a bit of protein, and zero gut drama. I started making my own at home: sushi rice, a pinch of salt, and whatever filling I have on hand (tuna, avocado, or just plain).

Here’s why they’re perfect for 100km rides:

  • Easy to eat one-handed. Wrap them in nori or plastic wrap, and you can nibble while pedaling.
  • No sugar crash. Rice gives you steady energy without the spike-and-crash of gels.
  • Customizable. Add sesame seeds, furikake, or even a dab of sriracha for flavor.

I usually pack three or four for a century ride. They’re compact, don’t squish, and taste way better than any packaged bar. If you’re serious about real food on the bike, this is the move. Pro tip: freeze them overnight, and they’ll thaw perfectly by the time you hit the halfway point.

The Day I Bonked 30km from Home

It was a perfect Saturday morning—sunny, no wind, legs feeling fresh. I set out for a 100km loop with just two bananas and a half-eaten granola bar. Big mistake. Around the 70km mark, my legs started feeling like lead. By 80km, I was fighting tunnel vision. At 85km, I pulled over, sat on a curb, and stared at my Garmin like it had personally betrayed me.

I had bonked. Hard. My brain was foggy, my hands were shaking, and the last 30km home felt like a death march. I ended up walking my bike to a gas station, buying a bag of gummy worms and a Coke, and sitting there for 20 minutes just to function. It was humiliating. I’d underestimated how much fuel a 100km ride actually demands—about 300-400 calories per hour, depending on intensity.

That day changed everything. I started planning my nutrition like I plan my route: with intention. Now I never leave home without at least 800 calories of real food in my pockets. Bonking is a rite of passage, but it’s one you only need to experience once to learn the lesson.

Building Your Own Ride Fuel Strategy

Everyone’s gut is different, so there’s no one-size-fits-all plan. But after years of trial and error, here’s what works for me on a 100km ride:

  • Eat before you’re hungry. Start fueling at the 30-minute mark, even if you feel fine. By the time you’re hungry, it’s too late.
  • Mix sweet and savory. I’ll pack a rice ball (savory) and a banana (sweet) for balance. Too much sugar makes me crash; too much salt makes me thirsty.
  • Hydrate with real stuff. Skip the neon sports drinks. Water with a pinch of salt and a squeeze of lemon works wonders. Or try a hydration tablet for electrolytes without the sugar bomb.
  • Test everything on shorter rides. Never try a new food on a 100km day. I learned that the hard way with a spicy peanut butter wrap that did not agree with me mid-climb.

If you’re still using gels and bars, I’m not judging. But if you’re curious about real food, start small. Pack one rice ball and one banana for your next 50km spin and see how you feel. Your stomach will thank you.

And hey, if you’re looking for a solid bike to test these strategies on, I’ve been loving my endurance road bike. It’s comfortable for long days and has plenty of pocket space for snacks. Pair it with a good saddle bag for storing your real food stash, and you’re golden.

Final Thoughts: Keep It Simple

Look, I’m not a nutritionist or a pro racer. I’m just a guy who loves riding and hates feeling like garbage on the bike. Real food isn’t flashy, but it works. Bananas are messy, rice balls take ten minutes to make, and you’ll never win a style award eating a sandwich on a climb. But you’ll finish your 100km ride feeling human, not hollow.

So next time you’re loading up for a long ride, skip the gel aisle and grab some real food. Your gut will thank you, your wallet will thank you, and you’ll probably enjoy the ride a whole lot more. Keep it simple, keep it real, and keep spinning.
