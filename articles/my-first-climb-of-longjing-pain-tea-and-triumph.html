My First Climb of Longjing: Pain, Tea, and Triumph

A rookie's honest take on climbing Longjing: too fast, too hard, but the tea fields made it epic.

The Start: Overconfident and Flying

I’ll be honest—I rolled up to the base of Longjing feeling like a god. It was 6:30 AM, the air was crisp, and my legs were fresh from three days of rest. I’d watched a dozen YouTube videos of guys crushing this climb on carbon aero bikes, and I figured, how hard can it be? I was riding my trusty Trek Domane AL 2 Disc, which I’ve had for two seasons now. It’s not a featherweight race machine, but I’ve put some solid miles on it.

The first two kilometers felt like a warm-up. My cadence was smooth, my breathing easy. I was even passing a few slower riders, nodding confidently. I thought, Yeah, I got this. The road wound through shady bamboo groves, and the gradient was gentle—maybe 4% max. I was flying, legs spinning like pistons. I even took a sip of water and checked my Garmin: 18.5 km/h average. Not bad for a climb, right? I was already planning my victory selfie at the top.

The Wall: Hitting the Third Switchback

Then came switchback number three. I remember the exact moment—the road kicked up hard, maybe 12% grade, and my legs went from “feeling good” to “what the hell just happened?” I dropped into my granny gear, but it was too late. The momentum was gone. My front wheel wobbled, and I nearly dabbed. I heard my own breath go from steady to ragged gasps.

My heart rate spiked to 178 bpm. I could feel the burn in my quads like someone was twisting a hot knife. I looked up and saw the road snaking upward forever. The bamboo was gone, replaced by exposed rock and the smell of hot tarmac. A local granny on a scooter zipped past me, giving me a pitying look. I wanted to scream, but I didn’t have the breath. I started zigzagging across the road, trying to find a line that wouldn’t kill me. It was ugly. It was humbling. And I was only halfway up.

Survival Mode: Grinding to the Top

At that point, I abandoned all pride. Here’s what I did to survive the last 3 kilometers:

  • Embrace the zigzag: I rode in wide S-curves, cutting the gradient by a couple of percent. It added distance but saved my legs.
  • Breathe like a bellows: I focused on deep, rhythmic breaths—in through the nose, out through the mouth. It kept my heart rate from redlining.
  • Pick a point, any point: I stopped looking at the top and started targeting the next road sign, tree, or crack in the pavement. Just 50 meters at a time.
  • Stand and sit: I alternated between seated grinding and standing surges. Each stand gave my lower back a break and recruited fresh glute muscles.

I also used a trick I learned from a pro friend: I shifted my weight forward on the saddle and pulled on the handlebars like I was trying to lift the bike. It seriously helped with traction on the steepest sections. And yes, I stopped once to catch my breath and take a gel. No shame.

The Reward: Tea Fields and Views

And then, suddenly, the road flattened out. I rounded one last bend, and the world opened up. The tea fields of Longjing spread out before me—perfectly manicured green terraces stretching across the hills, glowing in the morning light. The air smelled like fresh leaves and earth. I could hear birds, not traffic. I rolled to a stop, unclipped, and just stood there, chest heaving, grinning like an idiot.

I took out my phone and snapped a photo of my bike against the tea bushes. A farmer waved at me from a hundred meters away. I waved back. I felt like I’d earned something real. The descent was pure bliss—fast, winding, and with those views replaying in my head. I stopped at a roadside stall and bought a bag of fresh Longjing tea leaves. Cost me 30 yuan. Best souvenir ever.

If you’re thinking about tackling Longjing, I’d recommend upgrading to something like the Cannondale Synapse Carbon 105 for the extra climbing comfort—or at least swap your stock saddle for a Fizik Aliante R3. Trust me, your sit bones will thank you on those switchbacks.

Final Thoughts: The Honest Truth

Look, I’m not going to pretend I conquered Longjing like a pro. I crawled, I groaned, I questioned my life choices. But I also made it. And that’s the thing about these climbs—they don’t care about your Strava time or your bike’s price tag. They just ask you to keep pedaling. Some days you fly, some days you grind. Both count.

So if you’re planning your first big climb, just go. Bring water, bring patience, and don’t be afraid to zigzag. The tea at the top tastes better when you’ve earned it.
