My First 100km Ride: Pain, Cramp, and Pure Joy

My first 100km ride was brutal—cramps, pain, and doubt. But crossing the finish line made it all worth it.

The Big Goal

Let me tell you about the day I decided to stop being a "casual" cyclist and actually try something stupid. For months, I'd been doing 30-40km loops on weekends, feeling pretty good about myself. Then my buddy Dave casually mentioned his 100km Sunday ride like it was nothing. Something clicked. I wanted that. Not for Instagram clout or to impress anyone—I just wanted to know if I could do it.

So I planned it. Saturday morning, 6 AM. Flat route along the river, minimal traffic. Packed three water bottles, some energy gels, and a banana that I'd definitely regret eating later. My bike? A decent entry-level road bike I bought used—nothing fancy, but it had carried me through every ride so far. If you're thinking about upgrading, I'd recommend checking out this solid road bike that won't break the bank. But honestly, the bike matters less than your legs and your head.

The First 50km: Flying High

Those first two hours were pure magic. The sun was just coming up, the air was cool, and my legs felt like they belonged to a pro cyclist. I was averaging 28km/h, passing other riders with a smug little nod. The endorphins hit hard—I actually laughed out loud at one point, alone on an empty stretch of road. This is what people mean by "cycling high."

I stopped at a small café around 45km, feeling invincible. Ordered a coffee and a croissant like I'd earned it. Checked my phone—Dave had texted "How's it going?" I replied "Too easy." Famous last words. The next 5km were still smooth, but I noticed my quads starting to hum. Not a bad hum, just a "hey, we're working here" kind of hum. I ignored it. Big mistake.

The Wall: Cramps and Doubts

At 60km, everything fell apart. My left calf seized up like someone had twisted a rubber band too tight. I had to pull over, one foot on the ground, trying to stretch while traffic whizzed past. Then my right hamstring joined the party. I sat on the curb, sweating, questioning every life choice that led me here.

Here's what I learned the hard way:

  • Hydrate early, not late. I should have been sipping water from km 1, not chugging at km 50.
  • Eat real food halfway. Gels are fine, but a peanut butter sandwich would've saved me.
  • Don't skip warm-up stretches. I thought I was too cool for them. My calves disagreed.
  • Lower your ego. Pushing 28km/h for 100km is for pros. I should've settled at 24km/h.

If you're planning your first century, invest in proper gear. A good cycling computer helped me pace myself better later. And don't be like me—actually bring real snacks.

The Final Push: Mind Over Matter

After 10 minutes of pathetic stretching and self-pity, I got back on the bike. The cramps weren't gone, but they were manageable if I kept a steady cadence. Every pedal stroke from 60km to 80km was a negotiation with my brain: "Just one more kilometer. Then you can stop." I didn't stop. I couldn't. The thought of calling Dave and saying "I quit at 80km" was worse than the pain.

At 85km, something shifted. Maybe it was the endorphins again, or maybe my body just accepted the suffering. The last 15km felt almost easy—not fast, but smooth. I found a rhythm, focused on my breathing, and let the road carry me. I passed a group of riders and actually smiled. They smiled back. Cyclists get it.

The Finish Line: Sweet Satisfaction

When my computer clicked over to 100.0km, I pulled over, unclipped, and just stood there. My legs were shaking. My back ached. My hands were numb from gripping the bars. But I felt like I'd won something real. Not a race, not a competition—just a quiet victory over my own doubts.

I sat on a bench, drank the last of my water, and called Dave. "I did it." He laughed and said, "Told you it hurts." We made plans for a 120km ride next month. I groaned, but honestly? I'm already looking forward to it.

If you're thinking about your first 100km ride, just do it. It's going to hurt. You'll cramp. You'll question your sanity. But the feeling of rolling into that final kilometer makes every drop of sweat worth it. And if you want to be more comfortable, a decent saddle upgrade can save your butt—literally.
